# About this study

This page presents the **energy-system part** of the Integrated Marginal Abatement Cost Curve (iMACC) study for Ireland (2026–2050), prepared by UCC and University of Galway for DCEE.  
It summarises the cost and emissions impact of mitigation options across electricity, transport, buildings, services and industry.

## High-level description

The energy iMACC ranks mitigation measures by:

- **Abatement potential** (cumulative MtCO2e reduced vs BAU, 2026–2050)
- **Marginal abatement cost** (EUR/tCO2e), calculated as total system cost change divided by total emissions reduction

The tool is comparative: it shows scale and relative cost-effectiveness of measures under consistent assumptions. It is not a deployment forecast and does not by itself represent implementation feasibility, distributional effects, or fiscal impacts.

## How the energy study was done

The energy analysis uses the **TIMES-Ireland Model (TIM)**, a whole-energy-system optimisation model.

- A **BAU counterfactual** is used as reference (no additional mitigation deployment beyond current levels)
- Policy scenarios are run with emissions constraints and technology pathways
- For each measure, the study compares cumulative emissions and total system costs against BAU

System costs include investment and operating effects over 2026–2050.

## Energy scenario descriptions

### BAU (reference)

Counterfactual baseline used only for comparison. It is not intended as a projection of expected emissions.

### Scenario 1: Current policy ambition (WAM-type pathway)

Represents current policy direction with continued renewables and electrification, but lower ambition than a carbon-budget-constrained pathway.

### Scenario 2: Net-zero ambition (300 MtCO2eq carbon budget)

Energy-system pathway constrained by a **300 MtCO2eq** budget over 2026–2050, aligned with statutory 2030 sectoral ceilings and rapid decarbonisation thereafter.

## Technology deployment rates (energy system)

### Scenario 1 (Current policy ambition)

- **Onshore wind:** 8.9 GW by 2035; 10.5 GW by 2040 (maintained to 2050)
- **Offshore wind:** 1.5 GW by 2030; 5.2 GW by 2035; 9.3 GW by 2040 (maintained)
- **Solar PV:** 5.2 GW by 2035; 10.5 GW by 2040 (maintained)
- **Private BEVs:** 0.8 million by 2035; 1.47 million by 2040 (maintained)
- **Battery-electric trucks:** 26,000 by 2030; 157,000 by 2040 (maintained)
- **Biofuel blending:** E10 / B20 by 2030 (held constant)
- **Residential retrofit + heat pumps:** 270,000 retrofits and 407,000 heat pumps by 2040
- **District heating (services):** 0.2 TWh by 2030; 2.2 TWh by 2040 (maintained)

### Scenario 2 (Net-zero ambition, 300 Mt)

- **Onshore wind:** 8.8 GW by 2040
- **Offshore wind:** 17 GW by 2050
- **Solar PV:** 10 GW by 2050
- **Private BEVs:** 776,000 by 2030; 3.7 million by 2050
- **New private car sales:** 100% BEV share from 2029
- **Battery-electric trucks:** 792,000 by 2050
- **Industry electrification:** electricity reaches 77% of final energy use by 2050
- **District heating (residential):** 56,000 dwellings by 2030; 214,000 by 2050
- **Heat pumps (services):** 3.2 GW by 2050
- **District heating (services):** up to 10 GW in 2030–2040, ~7 GW by 2050
- **BECCS (power):** 500 MW investment in 2030, online by 2035
- **Residential retrofit + heat pumps:** ~180,000 retrofits and ~1 million heat pumps by 2050
- **Battery-electric buses:** 42,000 by 2050
- **Biomethane (energy):** 5.7 TWh by 2030 (held constant)
- **Biofuel blending:** E10 / B20 by 2030 (held constant)

## Interpretation notes

For energy, many high-impact measures in power and transport move to low or negative system cost over time due to fuel savings and electrification efficiency. This does not mean automatic uptake; delivery depends on grid, infrastructure, planning, supply chains, finance, and implementation capacity.
